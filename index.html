The roll-forever covered call, tested

Own the shares. Sell the monthly call where open interest is highest. If the stock runs past the strike, buy the call back and roll up and out for a net credit. Bank the premium into more shares. I coded the rules exactly, ran them on sixteen large caps for six years, and let real OCC open interest pick every strike.

01

How the strategy works

Twelve rules, reduced to a fixed engine. No discretion mid-cycle; every decision uses only data available on that day.

01

Own the stock first

The base asset is shares, not leverage. At the first usable snapshot the engine buys whole 100-share lots with the $100,000 starting cash and holds them for the life of the study (re-buying after any forced assignment).

Notes example: 4,300 NVDA shares → 43 covered-call contracts. Here each name starts at $100k, so lot count scales with price.

02

Sell the call where open interest is highest

Among listed calls roughly 1–12% out of the money with a live bid, write the strike with the largest open interest. That is the “magnet” the notes describe: where speculative demand has piled up, and where option premium is often richest.

Every write on this page used real OCC morning open interest (ThetaData), not a proxy. 100% of cycles selected the max-OI strike in band.

03

Target about one month of time

Candidate expiries sit between 13 and 70 days out. Prefer those with no earnings date inside the holding window; among those, take the expiry closest to 28 days. If every candidate spans earnings, sell through it — that option’s premium already carries the elevated earnings IV.

The chain source lists nothing under ~13 days to expiry, so the study cannot re-quote the short call in its final two weeks.

04

At expiry: keep the premium, or roll up and out

If the call finishes out of the money, pocket what remains and write the next month. If it finishes in the money, buy it back at intrinsic and sell a farther, higher (or equal) strike for at least as much premium — a net credit roll that keeps the shares.

Assignment is allowed only when no listed strike at or above the old one can cover the buyback. That is the honest cost of a sharp rally.

05

Reinvest premium into more lots

After each settlement, spare cash buys additional 100-share lots. Those new lots get covered the same cycle, so capacity compounds with the premium stream.

A second curve on the NVDA chart banks the same premium as cash instead, so you can see what compounding actually bought.

02

One cycle, end to end

Every month the engine walks the same decision tree. This is the whole strategy in one picture.

Most common

Expired

Stock finishes below the strike. Short call settles at zero. Premium stays in the account. Write the next month’s highest-OI call.

The notes’ move

Rolled

Stock finishes at or above the strike. Buy back at intrinsic, sell a higher/later strike whose premium covers the buyback. Cash never falls on the roll.

The honest loss

Assigned

No listed strike at or above the old one can fund the buyback (sharp rally, or the old strike falls off the ±30% listing window). Shares leave; re-buy at the new spot.

03

NVDA: premium compounds, the cap compounds faster

$100,000 start, mid fills, T-bill interest on idle cash, premium reinvested into more lots. Same marks as a dividend-reinvested buy-and-hold.

NVDA is the notes’ worked example, so it gets the full equity path. Across six years the engine collected $1.48M of gross call premium, 80% of cycles were profitable, and 2022’s crash cut the account by about −32% against roughly −50% for the shares alone. Ending equity: $752k with reinvestment, $687k when premium was banked as cash, and $3.00M for buy and hold. Twelve forced assignments sit inside those years — almost always in the months that made holding rich.

04

Sixteen names, same rules

No parameter tuning per stock. Open interest picks the strike; the roll rule is identical everywhere.

Median CAGR across the basket: +13.3% for the covered-call roll against +18.6% for buy and hold. The four names that beat holding — CSCO, PFE, T, INTC — were the flat or declining stocks. That is the classic covered-call profile, now with the open-interest magnet tested for real: income helps when the shares go nowhere; it costs you the best months when they run.

05

What the numbers do not hide

The pitch frames covered calls as safer than owning shares. Safer on the way down; expensive on the way up.

Upside is capped until a roll succeeds. A sharp rally can make the short call expensive to buy back; if no higher strike clears a net credit, the shares are called away and re-buying them costs the whole gap. That is exactly where the 117 forced assignments landed.

Downside is only partly offset. Premium collected in quiet months cushions a decline, but a large drop still hits the stock position. NVDA’s 2022 drawdown was roughly two-thirds as deep as buy and hold — better, not painless.

Open interest is not a price forecast. High OI shows where bets are concentrated. It does not guarantee the stock drifts to those strikes. The engine uses OI only as a strike-selection signal for inflated premium, not as a directional model.

Earnings and gaps still bite. Selling through an earnings window can collect juiced IV, but the move can exceed the premium. Rolling defers assignment risk; it does not erase it.

Modeling floor that flatters the strategy: expiring calls are bought back at exact intrinsic (zero time value, zero spread), because the chain source has no quotes under 13 DTE. Mid-cycle defensive rolls are not modeled for the same reason.
06

How the study was built

Every figure on this page comes from fixed rules and cached market data. Nothing was fit after the fact.

Data

Option chains: DoltHub post-no-preference/options — near-daily snapshots with bid, ask, IV and delta. Each snapshot lists roughly three expirations (~2, ~4, and ~7 weeks; nothing under ~13 days) and strikes within about ±30% of spot. Open interest: ThetaData OCC morning numbers for every snapshot date, 2020–2025, cached to SQLite. Underlyings, dividends, splits, and earnings from Yahoo Finance; cash earns the 13-week T-bill rate (^IRX).

Engine rules

  • Entry: buy floor(cash / (100 × spot)) lots; write one call per lot.
  • Strike: max open interest among calls with strike in [1.01, 1.12] × spot and bid > 0.
  • Expiry: DTE in [13, 70], prefer no earnings inside the window, then closest to 28 days.
  • Settlement: OTM → expire; ITM → buyback at max(0, settle − K), then roll to highest strike ≥ K_old whose premium covers the buyback (else assign and re-buy lots).
  • Fills: mid-quote on sells; intrinsic on expiry buybacks. A 25% half-spread haircut costs a few CAGR points and changes no conclusion.

What this data cannot test

True weekly cycles (no sub-13-DTE quotes). Mid-cycle rolls or stop-outs before expiry. Intraday assignment risk. The discretionary “sell a deep ITM call into earnings for a huge buffer” trade from the notes is not automated here.
