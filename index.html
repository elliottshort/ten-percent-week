I backtested the ten percent week

Someone sent me a screenshot pitching a weekly options trade on NVDA: buy a deep in-the-money call, sell one at the money against it, and collect 10% a week even if the stock goes nowhere. Instead of arguing about it, I rebuilt the trade and ran it against six years of real options data on sixteen stocks. Then I spent 3,456 more backtests trying to tune it until it worked. Everything I found is on this page.

01

What the trade actually is

The claims first, stated as generously as I can, because the arithmetic in the screenshot does check out.

With NVDA at $210.96, you buy the deep in-the-money $160 call for $5,027 and sell the at-the-money $210 call for $475 against it, both expiring in about a week. Net cost: $4,528 per contract set. If NVDA finishes anywhere above $210, the spread pays its full $5,000 width. That’s a +10.4% return in seven days, and you collect it even if the stock doesn’t move. The pitch says to repeat weekly, compound $45k into $50k, and keep harvesting “volatility premium” forever.

All of that is true. The screenshot just never shows you the left side of the payoff.

The long $160 call carries almost no time value. It’s a stock substitute with delta ≈ 1, which makes the position levered long NVDA at ~4.7× with the upside capped at +10.4%.

So the premium is real, but you’re not harvesting it for free. You’re getting paid to insure a levered position against a bad week. The rest of this page is about how often the bad week shows up.

02

It lasted seven weeks

I ran the trade as pitched, full account redeployed every cycle, starting January 2020 with $100,000.

My options source doesn’t carry expiries under ~13 days, so cycles run about two weeks instead of seven days. Same structure, same economics, half the compounding events (details in the methodology). The first two cycles delivered exactly what the screenshot promised: +19.3%, then +32.5%, up 58% in about a month. The account didn’t survive February.

And look at what killed it. Not some once-a-century crash. NVDA dropped 22.7% over two weeks, which it has done plenty of times since. With breakeven at −2.7% and roughly 4.7× leverage, one bad-but-ordinary stretch takes the whole account, and betting the full balance every cycle means your first bad fortnight is your last.

03

The other fifteen stocks

Maybe NVDA was just unlucky, so I ran the identical strategy on fifteen more large caps.

Thirteen of the sixteen all-in accounts went to zero. The three “survivors” (AMZN, WMT, CSCO) drew down 89–98% along the way, which I’d call surviving on a technicality. You can’t blame COVID for this either: NFLX sailed through March 2020 untouched and then died on a single earnings gap in January 2022. I also restarted every account in January 2021, skipping the crash entirely, and five names still went to zero: AMD and BA in 2022, INTC in 2024, DIS in April 2025.

04

Then I tried to fix it

108 configurations, with a wall between the years that pick the winner and the years that judge it.

I swept every lever the trade has: long-leg depth (76 / 85 / 92% of spot), short strike (at the money or 5% out), expiry (two weeks or a month), sizing (25 / 50 / 100% of equity), and two entry filters with an actual rationale behind them, skipping earnings fortnights and gating on trend. Every configuration got tuned on 2020–2022 and judged on 2023–2025 with fresh capital, across all sixteen names. If you let a strategy tune and grade itself on the same years, you can manufacture any result you want. That’s how pitches like this get made in the first place.

The best configuration I’d actually defend (92%-moneyness long leg, monthly expiry, quarter sizing, no filter) earned +7.3% in-sample and +26.1% out-of-sample. Look at what each “refinement” did, though: less leverage, longer cycles, smaller size. I asked the sweep to maximize returns and it spent 3,456 backtests turning the strategy back into a plain covered call.

The configs that did best out-of-sample (+33–40%) all had negative or ruinous in-sample records. Picking those now would just be hindsight.
05

What if we just skip COVID?

Fair objection: the account died in March 2020, so maybe this whole study is a COVID artifact. I reran everything starting January 2023.

This is the friendliest window the trade could ask for. No crash, three years of mostly-up market, and NVDA more than doubling every year. Even here, two of the sixteen all-in accounts still went to zero: AMD on July 26, 2024 and INTC two weeks later, both killed by an ordinary summer semiconductor selloff, no crisis anywhere in sight. The other fourteen survived with drawdowns between 24% and 92% along the way.

The surviving all-in accounts did post huge numbers where the stock ripped: +396% a year on NVDA, +142% on AMZN, +125% on MSFT. Before that looks like vindication, remember what the position is. A 4.7× levered long in a bull market prints money because it’s levered, not because it’s harvesting anything; a margin account would have done the same job. The same structure lost 23–42% a year on XOM, PFE, BA and DIS over the same period, and the “harvest” framing is nowhere in either outcome.

Sized down to 25% so it can actually survive, the trade lost to buy-and-hold on 15 of 16 names even in this window, with a median of +12.9% against +29.6%. The one win is PFE, where capping your upside happens to help because the stock went down. That’s the covered-call effect, which you can get without any of the leverage.

Holding the shares beat every harvest configuration I tested, and that’s before real-world fills get worse than mid-quote.

The 10% week is the maximum payoff of a bet, not a yield. To be fair to the pitch, 61% of NVDA cycles did pay in full, and the median fortnight was positive. None of that mattered, because one ordinary bad fortnight erases months of harvesting, and the sizing that makes the returns exciting is the same sizing that makes the damage permanent. After 3,456 attempts to find a version of this trade worth running, I can tell you it comes in two flavors: the ones that eventually blow up, and the ones that underperform a covered call.

07

How I ran all of this

Every number above is reproducible from the repo. This is how they were made, including what this data can’t test.

Data

Option chains come from the DoltHub post-no-preference/options dataset: near-daily snapshots with bid, ask, IV and delta. Each snapshot carries roughly three expirations (~2, ~4, ~7 weeks, nothing under ~13 days out) and strikes within about ±30% of spot. Underlying closes, dividends, splits and earnings dates come from Yahoo Finance, with the 13-week T-bill (^IRX) as the cash rate. I cache all of it to SQLite (~52,000 chain snapshots across 16 symbols), so every run after the first is fully offline.

Reconstructing the trade

Each cycle buys the call nearest 76% of spot (clamped to the deepest listed strike) and sells the call nearest the money, same expiry, at mid-quote. I hold to expiry, settle at intrinsic value against the closing price, and re-enter the same day. Contracts get floored to whole units, and idle cash earns nothing, which if anything flatters the sized variants less. I also ran a second fill model that pays 25% of the half-spread on each leg. It costs the strategy 2–6 CAGR points and changes no conclusion. Real two-legged weekly fills would be worse than either.

The one adaptation

The pitch uses 7-day expiries and the dataset’s shortest is ~14 days, so my cycles run two weeks. It’s the identical structure with half the compounding events and a slightly gentler risk profile per unit of time. If anything that flatters the pitch, since the true weekly version meets one more coin flip every month.

Splits, and a trap I fell into

Strikes are as-traded on their date, while cached closes are split-adjusted to the present, so the engine back-adjusts strikes with the cumulative product of splits after each date. Here’s the trap: you can’t date-slice the price series for windowed tests, because splits that happen after the window ends (NVDA 10:1 in 2024, WMT 3:1 in 2024, NFLX 10:1 in 2025) are baked into the closes but invisible to the slice. My first sweep pass silently dropped those three names in-sample before I caught it. The published numbers gate entries by date on full-period data instead. If you build backtests, watch for this one.

What counts as ruin

An account is ruined when it can no longer fund a single contract of the next cycle. All-in NVDA went from $100,000 to $15 in four cycles (Jan 24 to Mar 20, 2020). Annualizing a six-week −100% produces nonsense, so I report ruined runs as ruin dates, not CAGRs.

The sweep protocol

  • Grid: moneyness {76, 85, 92%} × short strike {ATM, 105%} × expiry {~14d, ~28d} × sizing {25, 50, 100%} × filter {none, skip-earnings, skip-earnings + trend gate} = 108 configs.
  • No look-ahead: the trend gate is a trailing 20-day regression t-statistic, and earnings dates are known in advance. Nothing in any filter uses future data.
  • The split: tuned on 2020–2022, judged on 2023–2025 with fresh $100k per name. Each config scores as its median CAGR across all 16 names, with ruins counted as −100%.
  • The damage: 39 configs ruined in-sample. At full sizing, 457 in-sample and 237 out-of-sample account deaths, and zero full-sizing configs survived both windows. Spearman between in-sample and out-of-sample rank: 0.58 overall, 0.20 within fixed sizing.

What this data can’t test

Three things. True 7-day cycles: no sub-13-DTE quotes exist in the source. Mid-cycle exits or stops: a position opened at 14 DTE has no quotes once it decays below the source’s floor, so everything is held to expiry. A stop-loss would change the shape of the returns, but you have to price one to claim it helps, not assume it. The GEX overlay: the pitch pairs this trade with dealer-gamma dashboards, and I have no historical dealer-positioning data to test timing with. I’ll note that a timing signal changes how often you hold the position, not what the payoff does to you when you’re wrong.

Reproducing it

The engine and drivers live in my buywrite-backtest repo: wheel_bt/harvest.py (86 lines, 5 tests), run_harvest.py, scripts/sweep_harvest.py, and scripts/export_site_data.py for this page’s numbers.
